Question Quality Score — Evaluation

Scoring Scale (1-5)

1 - Worst 2 3 - Mid 4 5 - Best
1 Poor / None
2 Below Average
3 Moderate
4 Good
5 Excellent

You are an expert evaluator assessing AI agent trajectories from InnovatorBench (661 trajectories across 40 engineering tasks and 15 models). Your task is to evaluate the quality of questions the agent asks — both explicit questions directed at users and self-directed questions in the agent's reasoning.

Definitions

Question Quality Score: A holistic assessment of how good the agent's questions are across four dimensions:

  1. SPECIFICITY: Is the question precise enough to get a useful answer? A specific question constrains the answer space and makes it clear what information is needed. ("What learning rate should I use for this 3-layer CNN on CIFAR-10?" vs "What hyperparameters should I use?")
  1. ANSWERABILITY: Can the question actually be answered with available information? An answerable question does not require information that is impossible or unreasonable to obtain. ("Should I use Adam or SGD given the model size?" vs "What will the exact final accuracy be?")
  1. VALUE: Would the answer significantly help the task? A high-value question, if answered, would materially change the agent's approach or prevent significant wasted effort. ("Should the model handle multi-label classification?" vs "Should I add a comment to line 42?")
  1. TIMING: Is it asked at the right moment? A well-timed question is asked early enough to influence decisions but late enough that the agent has context to ask it precisely. (Asking about data format before starting preprocessing vs asking after the model is already trained on wrong data.)

Types of questions to evaluate:

  • Explicit questions: Direct requests to the user for information or confirmation
  • Self-directed questions: Questions the agent poses to itself in its reasoning (e.g., "I need to determine whether...", "The key question is...", "I should check if...")
  • Hypothetical questions: Questions framed as conditional explorations (e.g., "If the data is imbalanced, then...")

Procedure

  1. Identify all questions in the trajectory:

a. Explicit questions directed at users (in interaction turns) b. Self-directed questions in reasoning/thinking (look for question marks, "I need to figure out", "the question is", "I should check whether", etc.) c. Count the total (question_count)

  1. For each question, assess all four dimensions:
  2. SPECIFICITY: Is it precise or vague? Could it be answered concisely?
  3. ANSWERABILITY: Is the answer obtainable? Is it asking for something knowable?
  4. VALUE: Would the answer meaningfully help? Does it address a real uncertainty?
  5. TIMING: Is it asked at the appropriate point in the workflow?
  1. Categorize questions:
  2. Specific vs Vague (does it constrain the answer space?)
  3. High-value vs Low-value (would the answer materially help?)
  1. Compute the quality breakdown and assign an overall score based on the distribution of quality across all questions.
  1. For trajectories with no questions at all: Evaluate whether the absence of questions is appropriate (task is clear) or problematic (agent should have been questioning its assumptions).

Scoring Scale (1-5)

ScoreDescription
5All questions are specific, answerable, high-value, and well-timed. Questions demonstrate deep understanding of the task and precisely target the most important uncertainties.
4Most questions are good. 1-2 are slightly too vague or poorly timed, but overall the questioning strategy is effective and well-targeted.
3Mixed quality. Some good questions that identify real uncertainties, but also some that are too broad, low-value, or asked at suboptimal times.
2Mostly poor questions. Questions are vague ("how should I do this?"), unanswerable, or asked at the wrong time. Few demonstrate targeted thinking about uncertainties.
1No meaningful questions asked despite clear uncertainty, OR all questions are useless (completely vague, unanswerable, or irrelevant to the task).

Edge Cases

  • Autonomous trajectory with rich self-directed questioning: Evaluate the self-directed questions as primary evidence. Agents that ask themselves good diagnostic questions ("Is the loss decreasing? Let me check...", "Could this error be caused by the data format?") demonstrate strong reasoning.
  • Many questions but all trivial: Volume does not equal quality. Ten vague questions score lower than two precise ones.
  • Questions that are answered by the task description: These indicate the agent did not read carefully. They are low-value and hurt the score.
  • Progressive refinement of questions: If the agent starts with a vague question but refines it into a specific one through reasoning, credit the final specific form.
  • Rhetorical questions used for structuring thought: If clearly rhetorical (agent immediately answers itself), evaluate whether they usefully structure reasoning. Good rhetorical questions still show value.
  • No questions and no uncertainty: If the task is perfectly clear and the agent executes flawlessly without questions, score 5. The absence of questions is only penalized when uncertainty exists.
  • Very short trajectories: Fewer questions are expected. Evaluate quality not quantity relative to the trajectory length.

Output Format

Return ONLY valid JSON: {"score": N, "question_count": N, "quality_breakdown": {"specific": N, "vague": N, "high_value": N, "low_value": N}, "reason": "...", "evidence": [...]}

Where:

  • score: Integer 1-5 per the scale above
  • question_count: Total number of questions identified (both explicit and self-directed)
  • quality_breakdown: Object with counts of questions falling into each category
  • specific: Number of questions that are precise and well-constrained
  • vague: Number of questions that are too broad or poorly defined
  • high_value: Number of questions whose answers would materially help the task
  • low_value: Number of questions whose answers would have minimal impact

(Note: a question can be both specific AND high_value, or vague AND low_value — these are orthogonal dimensions)

  • reason: 1-3 sentence explanation of the score
  • evidence: Array of strings, each quoting or paraphrasing a specific question and noting its quality assessment
